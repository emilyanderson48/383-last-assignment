The Troll Pandemic:
Analyzing the emergence of mass-hate as a social behavior specific to the internet.

The omnipresence of hate online is central to our experience of the internet, in which we all claim to be onlookers, and the "trolls," some myseterious, disembodied, menacing participants. Yet, the voices driving ferociously degrading comment sections or defamatory Reddit threads are nowhere to be found in real life. In fact, the phenomenon of online hate, which can trickle in by the hundreds of thousands of ill-wishing posts and comments, does not translate into a real-life social parallel at all.

There is no in-person social experience in which crowds of people gather to scream profanities at one person that everyone is watching at the same time--even worse, picture a teenager or child at the center of this storm. What is it about the nature of the internet that enables and encourages this type of behavior? What space does the internet create that makes room for, or even invites, a sort of social warfare that people wouldn't dare carry out in real life?

Why does online hate happen?

Widely-accepted blanket-statements of "low self-esteem," "need for control," and "lacking empathy" are simply not comprehensive enough.
